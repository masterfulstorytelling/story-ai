# Corporate Storytelling Evaluation Tool - User Personas & Pain Points

## Overview

This tool serves people in acute business crises - individuals who wake up at 3am in a panic because a critical business function is failing due to unclear storytelling.

## Persona 1: The CRO with the Champion Problem

### Profile
**Name:** David Chen  
**Title:** Chief Revenue Officer  
**Company:** 400-person enterprise infrastructure company  
**Industry:** B2B SaaS / Cloud Computing  

### The 3am Moment
"We lost the Citibank deal. Again. Same pattern - their VP of Infrastructure was ready to sign, went to his CFO for budget approval, and the CFO said 'I don't understand what makes you different from AWS.' We've lost $8M in deals this quarter because our champions can't sell us internally."

### Business Pain Metrics
- **Win rate:** 65% with technical buyers ‚Üí 23% after executive review
- **Sales cycle:** 9 months (should be 6)
- **Lost pipeline:** $43M this year
- **Deal stall point:** CFO/CTO budget approval
- **Sales team frustration:** "We can't arm our champions"

### Trigger Moments
- Post-mortem meeting where CFO literally said "what do they do?"
- Sales VP reports: "Our champions love us but can't explain us"
- Board asks: "Why are we losing deals at the final stage?"
- Another $4M deal lost at executive review

### What He Needs to Learn
- ‚úì Can a non-technical executive understand what we do from our materials?
- ‚úì Are we writing for the champion or the budget holder?
- ‚úì What specific language loses CFOs?
- ‚úì How do we compare to competitors who ARE winning CFO approval?

### User Stories

**Story 1: The Budget Authority Test**
> "As a CRO whose technical champions can't get budget approval, I want to know whether our materials can be understood by non-technical executives, so that I can stop losing deals at the CFO/CTO level."

**Acceptance Criteria:**
- Report explicitly tests: "Can a CFO understand this?"
- Shows technical level mismatch between audiences
- Identifies specific jargon that loses business readers
- Quantifies the gap (e.g., "Technical audience: 82/100, CFO audience: 30/100")
- Provides CFO-friendly language alternatives

**Story 2: The Champion Enablement Package**
> "As a sales leader, I want to know what specific phrases confuse non-technical buyers, so that I can train my team to arm champions with better language."

**Acceptance Criteria:**
- Lists problematic phrases with explanations of why they fail
- Shows what each audience understands vs doesn't
- Provides before/after examples
- Downloadable for sales training
- Includes "translate technical to business" examples

**Story 3: The Deal Post-Mortem Evidence**
> "As a CRO explaining to the board why we're losing deals, I want objective evidence of our messaging problems, so that I can justify investment in fixing this."

**Acceptance Criteria:**
- Quantified scores that show the problem clearly
- Connection to business impact ("This is costing you $8-15M annually")
- Competitor comparison showing how others do it better
- Specific examples from our materials that fail

### What Success Looks Like
After reading the report, David:
- Understands EXACTLY why deals are stalling
- Has evidence to show the board ("Our CFO clarity score is 30/100")
- Knows what needs to change (specific phrases to fix)
- Sees this is complex enough to need expert help
- Books a call with Feedforward

## Persona 2: The CHRO with the Talent Problem

### Profile
**Name:** Jennifer Martinez  
**Title:** Chief Human Resources Officer  
**Company:** 400-person fintech company  
**Industry:** Financial Technology  

### The 3am Moment
"We just lost another finalist to Stripe. Third one this month. Our comp was competitive - actually 15% higher. But in the debrief, the candidate said 'I couldn't figure out what made your company special.' We're paying 30% above market to close people because they're not compelled by the mission - they're just negotiating."

### Business Pain Metrics
- **Offer acceptance rate:** 52% (should be 75%+)
- **Premium to close:** 28% above initial offer
- **Talent cost overrun:** $2.4M this year
- **Employee referral rate:** 8% (should be 25%+)
- **Exit interview insight:** "I never really understood our value prop"

### Trigger Moments
- Lost third finalist this month to competitor with lower comp
- Recruiting firm says: "Your careers page doesn't differentiate you"
- Candidate literally says: "I couldn't tell what made you special"
- CEO asks: "Why are we paying so much above market?"
- Exit interview: Engineer leaving after 8 months can't articulate what the company does

### What She Needs to Learn
- ‚úì Can a talented engineer understand why we're special?
- ‚úì Do our materials convey what it's like to work here?
- ‚úì Are we generic in a way that makes us forgettable?
- ‚úì Can current employees explain us to friends they want to refer?

### User Stories

**Story 4: The Recruit Excitement Test**
> "As a CHRO losing finalists even with competitive comp, I want to know whether our story makes us memorable and compelling, so that I can stop overpaying to close candidates who aren't truly excited."

**Acceptance Criteria:**
- Memorability score with specific examples
- Shows where we sound like every other company
- Identifies what's missing: values, personality, what makes us special
- Tests "Would this excite a talented recruit?"
- Compares to competitors who ARE exciting candidates

**Story 5: The Employee Referral Test**
> "As a talent leader with low referral rates, I want to know if our employees can easily explain what makes us special, so that they can successfully refer talented friends."

**Acceptance Criteria:**
- "Elevator pitch" test: Can someone summarize us in 30 seconds?
- Shows where language is too complex for casual explanation
- Tests whether employees can articulate our value
- Provides simple, memorable alternatives
- "Can you explain this to a friend at a party?" score

**Story 6: The Retention Understanding Test**
> "As a CHRO with rising turnover, I want to know if our internal story helps employees understand why their work matters, so that they stay engaged and committed."

**Acceptance Criteria:**
- Tests whether importance/meaning is clear
- Shows abstract vs concrete language about impact
- Evaluates whether non-experts can understand our value
- Identifies where mission gets lost in jargon

### What Success Looks Like
After reading the report, Jennifer:
- Understands why candidates aren't excited (memorability: 18/100)
- Sees specific language that makes us forgettable
- Has evidence for CEO about why talent costs are high
- Knows what needs to change in careers page, employee communications
- Books a call to fix messaging before next hiring push

- Exit interview: "I didn't understand how my work mattered"
- Employee says at party: "I work in healthcare IT" (instead of "I help save lives")

### What He Needs to Learn
- ‚úì Do our internal materials convey importance and meaning?
- ‚úì Can employees easily articulate what we do and why it matters?
- ‚úì Is our language too abstract when it should be concrete?
- ‚úì Do employees feel proud to explain their work to others?

### User Stories

**Story 7: The Pride Test**
> "As a people leader whose employees don't understand the value of what we do, I want to know whether our internal story conveys importance and meaning, so that I can improve morale and reduce turnover."

**Acceptance Criteria:**

**Story 9: The Mission Connection Test**
> "As a people leader with falling engagement, I want to know if our materials connect daily work to meaningful impact, so that employees feel their work matters."

**Acceptance Criteria:**
- Tests whether importance is conveyed for employee audience
- Shows where abstract language loses emotional connection
- Identifies missing links between "what we do" and "why it matters"
- Provides concrete examples of impact

### What Success Looks Like
After reading the report, Robert:
- Understands why employees can't articulate value (clarity for employees: 53/100, importance: 22/100)
- Sees specific language that's too abstract
- Has evidence for CEO about morale problem root cause
- Knows what to change in internal comms, all-hands, onboarding
- Books a call to overhaul employee-facing story

## Cross-Persona Patterns

### Common Thread: The "Champion Can't Sell You Internally" Problem

All three personas face versions of the same issue:
- **CRO:** Technical champion can't convince CFO
- **CHRO:** Employee can't convince friend to join
- **People Leader:** Employee can't explain value to family/friends

### The Core Insight
Your story works for ONE audience (the technical expert, the insider) but fails for ANOTHER audience (the decision-maker, the outsider, the non-expert).

### What They All Need
1. **Quantified evidence** of the problem (scores)
2. **Specific examples** of what's failing (citations)
3. **Audience-specific assessment** (not one-size-fits-all)
4. **Business impact** (what this is costing them)
5. **Proof this is complex** (so they need expert help)

## User Journey: From Crisis to Call

### Stage 1: Crisis Recognition (Before They Find Us)
- Something specific goes wrong (lost deal, lost candidate, engagement drop)
- They suspect messaging is part of the problem
- They're looking for validation or evidence

### Stage 2: Discovery
- Find Feedforward through Adam's content
- See the evaluation tool
- Think: "This could give me the evidence I need"

### Stage 3: Submission (30 seconds)
- Quick form: URL + email
- Optional: files, specific audience
- Low barrier: No account, no commitment
- Expectation: "I'll get something useful in 10 minutes"

### Stage 4: The Wait (5-10 minutes)
- Confirmation email: "We're analyzing..."
- Anxiety: "What if it's too generic?"
- Hope: "What if this nails the problem?"

### Stage 5: Report Arrival
**First 30 seconds (Executive Summary):**
- See their specific business problem named
- See quantified scores
- Think: "Holy shit, they got it" or "This is wrong"

**Next 5 minutes (Skimming):**
- Look for: Real citations from their content?
- Look for: Specific to them or generic?
- Feel: Defensive but also validated

**Next 10-15 minutes (Deep Read):**
- Read detailed findings
- See competitor comparison
- Realize: "This is more complex than I thought"
- Think: "I can't fix this myself"

### Stage 6: Decision Point
**If the report is good:**
- Forward to colleagues ("See? This is what I've been saying")
- Forward to CEO ("This explains why we're losing deals")
- Book call with Feedforward

**If the report misses:**
- Dismiss it
- Don't share
- Lost opportunity

### Critical Success Factors
The report must:
1. **Name their specific crisis** (not generic)
2. **Show evidence** (real citations, not made up)
3. **Quantify the problem** (scores that are shocking)
4. **Prove complexity** (thorough enough to be intimidating)
5. **Offer hope** (this is fixable with expert help)

## Jobs to Be Done

When someone uses this tool, they're hiring it to:

### Primary Jobs
1. **Validate a suspicion** ("I think our messaging is broken")
2. **Build a case** ("I need evidence to convince leadership")
3. **Understand the problem** ("I know something's wrong but can't articulate what")
4. **Find the solution** ("Who can help me fix this?")

### Secondary Jobs
1. **Benchmark against competitors** ("Are we worse than others?")
2. **Measure progress** ("Did our rebrand help?")
3. **Educate their team** ("Here's what good storytelling looks like")

### The Job They're NOT Hiring It For
- Learn how to fix it themselves (they want to hire you, not DIY)
- Get generic best practices (they want specific assessment)
- Save money (they're willing to pay for expertise)

## Anti-Personas (Who This Doesn't Serve)

### The DIY-er
- Wants to learn how to do this themselves
- Looking for a checklist or template
- Not willing to pay for expertise
- **Why they won't convert:** Looking for free education, not paid help

### The Skeptic
- Doesn't believe storytelling matters
- Just wants "the facts" to speak for themselves
- Thinks marketing is fluff
- **Why they won't convert:** Doesn't value what we offer

### The Broke Startup
- Can't afford consulting
- Needs cheap tools
- Will DIY everything
- **Why they won't convert:** Can't pay our rates

### The Enterprise Bureaucrat
- No authority to hire consultants
- Just gathering information
- Months-long procurement process
- **Why they won't convert:** Too slow, too many stakeholders
